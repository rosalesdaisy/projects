Optometrist

by Daisy Rosales

Job Description

An optometrist evaluates a patient's vision. It tests how well the patient perceives depth and color. It also checks the patient's concentration. They diagnose vision problems, eye diseases, and other medical conditions.

While treating the patient, the optometrist maintains a patient record by taking notes. They also advise patients on eye health and safety. For example, diabetics may develop eye problems as their disease worsens. These patients can receive advice on how to keep their eyes healthy.

Education and Training

If you want to become an optometrist, you can start preparing in high school. Try to take as many advanced math and science courses as possible. To become an optician, you need good grades in these subjects.

Before applying to optometry school, she must complete at least three years of undergraduate studies. As part of the program, you will be required to complete certain courses in Science, Mathematics and English. Most applicants have a bachelor's degree.

Earnings

The income of optometrists ranges from about $57,000 to over $188,000 per year. The median income is about $112,000 per year.

Core Tasks

  • Perform vision tests and analyze results.
  • Diagnose sight problems, such as nearsightedness or farsightedness, and eye diseases, such as glaucoma.
  • Prescribe eyeglasses, contact lenses, and other visual aids, and if state law permits, medications.

Workplace Conditions

  • Optical stores
  • Large stores with an optical center
  • Hospitals
  • Eye product manufacturers
  • Health care agencies
  • Insurance companies

Hiring Opportinities

Future Growth and Demands

Optometrist employment is projected to grow by 10% from 2021 to 2031, a faster pace than the average for all occupations. Approximately 1,700 optometrist vacancies are projected each year on average over the next decade. Many of these vacancies can arise from the need to replace workers who transition to other occupations or retire.
